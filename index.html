The Man. The Legend.

Who Is
Rickey Henderson?

Born on Christmas Day, 1958, in Chicago, Illinois, Rickey Henley Henderson grew up to become the greatest leadoff hitter in baseball history — a force of nature at the top of the lineup for 25 seasons across nine teams.

He made his MLB debut with the Oakland Athletics in 1979 and would go on to rewrite the record books. A menace on the basepaths, a disciplined hitter with genuine power, and an outfielder with a cannon for an arm, Rickey was a five-tool player who made every single at-bat — and every moment on base — must-see television.

He was inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame in 2009, receiving votes on 94.8% of ballots — one of the highest percentages in history.

"Rickey Henderson is the greatest player I've ever seen in my life. He could run, hit for average, hit for power, and play outstanding defense. He was the complete package."
— Tony La Russa, Hall of Fame Manager
"Don't talk to Rickey when Rickey is hitting. Rickey is thinking."

A 25-Year Journey

Career
Timeline

1979
MLB Debut — Oakland Athletics
Debuted at age 20, immediately flashing the speed and instincts that would define his career.
1980
100 Stolen Bases — First Time
Stole 100 bases in a single season for the first time, announcing himself to the baseball world.
1982
Breaks Single-Season SB Record
Stole 130 bases, obliterating Lou Brock's record of 118 set in 1974.
1985
Traded to the New York Yankees
Joined the Bronx Bombers in one of the most high-profile trades of the decade.
1989
Returns to Oakland — World Series Champion
Won the World Series with the A's, sweeping the San Francisco Giants in the earthquake-delayed series.
1990
AL MVP Award
Won the American League Most Valuable Player award, cementing his status as the game's best player.
1991
All-Time Stolen Base Record
Surpassed Lou Brock's all-time stolen base record of 938. Lou Brock was in the stands to witness it.
2001
All-Time Runs Scored Record
Broke Ty Cobb's career runs scored record, set back in 1928, crossing the plate for the 2,246th time.
2003
Final MLB Season
Played his final MLB game at age 44, finishing a 25-year career that touched nine franchises.
2009
Baseball Hall of Fame Induction
Inducted on 94.8% of ballots — one of the greatest induction percentages in Hall of Fame history.
